2025: Exeter's worst year on record — 292 closures

Exeter recorded 292 retail business closures in 2025 — versus just 33 in 2015 and 27 in 2019. This is not a COVID spike; it is a compound post-COVID collapse driven by business rates, energy costs, and permanent online shift. The 2025 total is nearly 4× the worst pre-COVID year (82 in 2018).

Devon: 1,708 closures in 2025 — a complete structural collapse

Across all 10 Devon councils, 2025 saw 1,708 retail business closures. North Devon (280), South Hams (280) and Torridge (336) all exceeded their entire 2015–2019 five-year totals in a single year. This is not a high street problem — it's a county-wide economic emergency.

Online reached 27.3% — the new structural floor

ONS J4MC series shows internet retail at 27.3% of all UK sales in 2025 — more than double the 12.5% of 2015. The COVID peak (31% in 2021) partially unwound, but the floor has permanently shifted. Every percentage point of online migration takes roughly £5bn/year out of physical retail nationally.

2016–2019: Structural decay predating every crisis

Brexit uncertainty (2016), online acceleration, and unsustainable rents drove UK closures from 16,210 to 46,076 before COVID arrived. Exeter's 2018 spike (82 panel closures) predated both COVID and the energy crisis. The structural forces were already terminal for many retailers.

2020–2021: Furlough deferred the cliff, it didn't prevent it

COVID closures in 2020 were suppressed by £70bn+ in government support. UK panel closures actually fell from 46k (2019) to 37k (2020). But 2021 saw 66k — a 78% surge as zombie businesses finally collapsed. Devon total 2021 (533) was already 40% above the 2019 figure of 380.

2022–2025: The four-force compound collapse

Energy (electricity +98% above 2015 by 2022), CPI (9.1% in 2022), business rates (+140% in 2024), and NLW (£12.21/hr 2025) — all four hitting simultaneously. Devon's 2025 total of 1,708 is 3× 2019's 556. Exeter's 292 in 2025 is 8.8× its 2015 figure.

What the data says about 2026

January 2026 shows 2 Exeter closures and 33 Devon closures already — partial month. NLW rose again to £12.71/hr in April 2026 and NI thresholds shifted further. Centre for Retail Research forecast 17,349 national closures for 2025 (already confirmed to be tracking higher). The structural forces show no sign of reversal.

COVID's Impact — and Why the Crisis Kept Getting Worse After

COVID did not create the retail crisis — it suppressed it artificially, then released it with compound interest. The real damage came from permanent behavioural shifts, not the initial shock.

Why the 2020 dip was dangerous — deferred, not prevented

UK-wide moratorium on winding-up orders (June 2020 – March 2022) legally prevented creditors from forcing businesses into insolvency. Combined with furlough and bounce-back loans, it artificially held 2020 closures below 2019 levels. When support ended, pent-up failures hit — Devon jumped from 383 (2020) to 533 (2021) to 585 (2022) in three consecutive years.

Online didn't peak and fall — it found a new floor

The ONS J4MC data shows online retail at 12.5% in 2015, 19.1% in 2019, peaking at 31% in 2021, and settling at 26–28% since. This means approximately 7–8 percentage points of UK retail spend has permanently migrated online since 2019. For Devon's 9,500 retail businesses, this equates to roughly £400–500m of annual turnover that no longer flows through physical premises.

The AWE trap: wages rose but retail prices couldn't

Average Weekly Earnings rose from £482/wk (2015) to £728/wk (2025) — a 51% increase. For independent retailers employing 2–3 people, this added £20,000–30,000/year in payroll costs. Simultaneously, consumers became price-sensitive due to their own cost-of-living pressures, preventing retailers from passing costs on. This scissors effect — rising costs, capped revenues — closed hundreds of Devon businesses.

What COVID permanently changed about how Devon shops

Footfall in UK city centres was still 10% below 2019 levels by 2023. The shift to hybrid working reduced lunch-hour trade in Exeter city centre. Online grocery delivery normalised, reducing incidental high street visits. Each behavioural shift is individually small; collectively they represent a permanent restructuring of how Devon residents engage with physical retail that no policy can fully reverse.

Torridge — 112× rise from 2015 to 2025

Torridge (Bideford, Holsworthy, Great Torrington) recorded just 3 retail closures in 2015. By 2025 that was 336 — a 112-fold increase. Rural North Devon is experiencing its worst retail collapse on record. Without the anchor of a major city centre, Torridge's retail ecosystem is particularly fragile to the combined pressure of online competition, rising costs, and population ageing.

North Devon — 280 closures in 2025

Barnstaple is North Devon's retail hub. 280 closures in 2025 compared to just 7 in 2015 and 28 in 2019. This 10× post-COVID surge reflects the collapse of the region's independent retail base. Barnstaple's Pannier Market and Butchers Row — historic retail destinations — face existential pressure from online competition and declining tourist footfall post-COVID.

Teignbridge's 2023 spike (247) — a structural warning

Teignbridge (Newton Abbot, Dawlish, Teignmouth) recorded 247 closures in 2023 — its highest ever, far above the county pattern. This spike likely reflects specific cluster insolvencies alongside the structural retail decline. Newton Abbot's town centre has been one of Devon's most challenged retail environments, with anchor vacancies predating COVID.

What Exeter's relative stability tells us

While Exeter's absolute 2025 figure (292) is high, its share of Devon closures (17%) is actually lower than its economic weight might suggest. Exeter benefits from university footfall, tourism (cathedral quarter), and relative economic resilience. The crisis hitting rural Devon harder per capita suggests that Exeter's BID strategy, if effective, could serve as a model for the county — making the InExeter BID's evidence base nationally significant.

Books & Media and Electronics lead Exeter closures — both facing 40%+ online penetration

Books & Media (17 closures) and Electronics (17 closures) are the top sectors in Exeter. Both face online penetration above 40% — meaning nearly half their potential market now transacts digitally. These were precisely the sectors that appeared sustainable pre-2015 but have seen structural collapse since. The closures include both independent specialists and small chains.

Health & Beauty: 3.4yr avg lifespan — fastest failing sector

Health and Beauty businesses in Exeter close after an average of just 3.4 years. These are predominantly independent beauty salons and cosmetics retailers opening into a market already dominated by online-first brands (ASOS Beauty, Cult Beauty, direct-to-consumer) and large chain discounters. New entrants face an almost impossible competitive environment from day one.

Butchery & Deli: 15yr+ avg — these are legacy losses

Ford Bros Butchery closed in 2017 after 52 years of trading — the longest-lived business in our dataset. W.H. Hicks Ltd (14.3 years), Matford Brook Ltd. These are not failed new businesses; they are multi-decade community anchors finally unable to compete with supermarket meat counters and changing food habits. Their closure has a different character — and a much greater community impact — than a 2-year beauty salon closing.

Sector mix reveals Exeter's retail character

Exeter's closure mix shows a city with a strong independent specialist base (antiques, second-hand, music, news & stationery) rather than a chain-dominated centre. These independents are simultaneously the city's distinctive retail offer and its most vulnerable businesses. Protecting independent specialist retail in Exeter is not nostalgia — it's the economic argument for why people drive to Exeter rather than shopping on Amazon.

Electricity +98% above 2015: the most acute single shock

Electricity CPIH hit 197.52 in 2022 — 98% above the 2015 baseline of 99.99. For a retail unit paying £400/month in 2015, this meant £790/month in 2022 energy costs. Gas was even worse (215 = +107%). Many Devon retailers had fixed-term energy contracts that expired precisely into the 2022 energy crisis peak, creating an immediate cliff-edge in operating costs with no ability to adjust pricing.

The 12-month lag: inflation destroys retailers the year after it peaks

CPI rate peaked at 9.05% in 2022. Devon closures then accelerated in 2023 (708) and surged in 2024 (955) and 2025 (1,708). This 12-18 month lag reflects the lifecycle: businesses exhaust cash reserves, exhaust credit, exhaust personal savings — and only then appear in dissolution data. The 2025 Devon closure record is partly the deferred consequence of 2022–2023 inflation.

Petrol at 164p/litre (2022) — the overlooked rural Devon factor

Devon is a predominantly rural county with poor public transport. Fuel costs are not a background factor — they directly affect consumer footfall. When petrol hit 164p/litre (2022) vs 111p in 2015, Devon residents made fewer non-essential shopping trips. Smaller town retailers and village shops were disproportionately affected. North Devon and Torridge's severe 2025 figures partly reflect accumulated consumer behaviour change driven by fuel costs.

AWE +51% — why rising wages didn't save retail

Average Weekly Earnings rose from £482 to £728 between 2015 and 2025. This should, in theory, support retail spending. But the gains were uneven — concentrated in professional and technical sectors rather than the customer bases of Devon's independent retail. Meanwhile, retailers themselves paid higher wages without proportional revenue growth. The consumer who earns more tends to spend online; the retailer who pays more doesn't benefit from their own wage inflation.
